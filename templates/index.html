Vascular Resistance — Where Is SVR Generated?
🔷 Anesthesia Pearl — Venodilation Comes First
Most anesthetic agents (propofol, volatile agents, neuraxial) cause venodilation before arterial vasodilation. Since 64% of blood resides in veins, venodilation dramatically reduces preload before SVR changes significantly. Phenylephrine restores venous tone → restores preload. This is why vasopressors work preferentially on the largest volume reservoir in the body.
High-Pressure System (Supply Function)
  • Aorta → major arteries → arterioles (primary resistance)
  • Primary determinants: SVR and cardiac output
  • Monitored via: Arterial line, MAP, pulse pressure
  • Afterload management critical in LV dysfunction
  • Aortic cross-clamp: supraceliac increases SVR 40–80%
Low-Pressure System (Reservoir Function)
  • Systemic veins + right heart + pulmonary vasculature
  • Most sensitive to: anesthetic-induced venodilation
  • Monitored via: CVP, PAP, PCWP (PA catheter)
  • Trendelenburg restores venous return acutely
  • Vasopressin, phenylephrine work primarily here
Key Exam Concept: During AAA repair, supraceliac cross-clamping causes a massive afterload spike (SVR ↑40–80%) requiring deep anesthesia or vasodilator infusion (NTG/SNP). "Declamping shock" occurs upon clamp release — ischemic metabolite washout from lower extremities + acute preload reduction. Anticipate and treat proactively.

⚠ Critical Warning — Radial Artery Before CABG
If the radial artery is planned as a CABG conduit (especially in younger patients or bilateral mammary contraindicated), do NOT place an arterial line in that arm. Coordinate with the surgical team before induction. Document Allen's test: compress radial → hold 30 sec → release → color return in <7 seconds = adequate ulnar collateral. ≥7 sec or incomplete filling = insufficient collateral — do not use that radial for conduit or A-line.

Aortic Segments — Surgical Anatomy
  • Ascending (5 cm): Aortic cannulation, cross-clamp in CABG, aortic valve surgery
  • Arch: Arch surgery requires DHCA ± ACP; brachiocephalic, L CCA, L subclavian branches
  • Descending thoracic: Intercostal arteries → spinal cord perfusion (TEVAR: risk of paraplegia)
  • Abdominal: Bifurcates L4 → common iliacs; AAA cross-clamp levels determine hemodynamic impact
Mnemonic — Aortic Branches (from arch): "Brachiocephalic trunk, Left Common Carotid, Left Subclavian" — BLS (Basic Life Support). In 0.5% of patients, the left vertebral artery arises directly from the arch between LCC and L subclavian.
Venous Anatomy & Central Venous Access
Site-specific anatomy, complication profiles, and ultrasound-guided approach considerations.
Internal Jugular Vein — Anatomy
  • Runs within carotid sheath: IJV + CCA + Vagus nerve (CN X)
  • At cricoid level (C4): IJV lies anterolateral to CCA
  • At clavicular level: IJV may be directly anterior to CCA — highest risk zone for carotid puncture
  • Right side preferred: Direct path to SVC; avoids thoracic duct; higher success rate
  • Ultrasound: IJV is compressible; CCA is non-compressible — key distinguishing feature
IJV Access Technique Tips
  • Trendelenburg 15–20°: Distends IJV significantly — increases target size
  • Valsalva: Further distends during spontaneous breathing
  • Enter between sternal and clavicular heads of SCM at 45°
  • Aim toward ipsilateral nipple; needle at 30–45° to skin
  • Confirm venous blood (non-pulsatile, dark) before advancing wire
  • Use real-time ultrasound — standard of care
⚠ Left-Sided Central Lines
The left IJV and left subclavian drain via the left brachiocephalic vein, which makes a 90° turn — higher catheter malposition risk. The thoracic duct drains at the left jugulovenous angle → injury causes chylothorax. Always prefer the RIGHT side for central venous access whenever clinically feasible.
Subclavian Vein — Anatomy
  • Courses from axillary vein (at lateral border of 1st rib) to brachiocephalic vein
  • Lies anterior to the anterior scalene muscle
  • Subclavian artery lies posterior to the anterior scalene — separated by this muscle
  • The vein is NOT compressible if arterial puncture occurs (behind clavicle)
  • Highest PTX risk of all CVC sites (2–3%) — needle aimed toward lung apex
  • Lowest infection rate — preferred for long-term CVC need
When to Choose Subclavian
  • Long-term CVC (TPN, chemotherapy, chronic medications)
  • Immunocompromised patients (infection risk reduction)
  • Maximum patient comfort — no neck immobility
  • Avoid in: coagulopathy, mechanical ventilation (PTX risk), bilateral pulmonary disease
  • Never bilateral subclavian attempts — non-compressible artery
⚠ Subclavian Arterial Injury Cannot Be Compressed
Unlike the carotid or femoral artery, the subclavian artery lies posterior to the clavicle and 1st rib — manual compression is impossible. Arterial puncture can cause massive hemothorax. If PTX occurs on one side and the patient requires PPV, bilateral PTX is immediately life-threatening. Abort and reassess if resistance is met or pulsatile blood is obtained.

Cardiac Anatomy — Chambers, Surfaces & TEE Correlation
Heart positioning, chamber relationships, pericardium, and standard intraoperative TEE windows.
Heart Orientation — Clinically Critical Points
  • Apex: Left, inferior, anterior → 5th ICS, midclavicular line
  • Base (posterosuperior): Receives pulmonary veins and great vessels
  • RV: Predominantly anterior — surgeon's first view; forms sternocostal surface
  • LV: Lateral and posterior — systemic pump; forms the apex
  • LA: Posterosuperior, directly anterior to esophagus → best TEE access to LA
  • RA: Right lateral surface; receives SVC superiorly, IVC inferiorly
Pericardium
  • Fibrous pericardium: Tough outer layer; fused to diaphragm; limits acute cardiac dilation
  • Serous pericardium: Parietal (lines fibrous) + Visceral (epicardium); ~50 mL normal fluid
  • Transverse sinus: Posterior to Ao/PA, anterior to SVC — surgeons pass snares here for great vessel control
  • Oblique sinus: Blind recess behind LA between pulmonary veins; relevant in pericardiocentesis approach
⚠ Cardiac Tamponade — Induction is High-Risk
Rapid accumulation (hemopericardium post-cardiac surgery): tamponade at 100–200 mL. Slow accumulation: compensation possible at 1000+ mL. Induction in tamponade → loss of sympathetic tone → loss of compensatory tachycardia + vasoconstriction → cardiovascular collapse.

Use KETAMINE for induction. Maintain HR and preload. Have surgeon scrubbed at the table before induction whenever possible. Keep epinephrine drawn up. Do not give large doses of volatile agents.
Precordial Auscultation — Anatomical vs. Auscultatory Location
Critical Teaching Point: Auscultation areas do NOT correspond to the anatomical location of the valves. Heart sounds are transmitted downstream with blood flow. This is why aortic stenosis is best heard at the right 2nd ICS (downstream from the valve toward the aorta), not at the valve's true position.

🔷 TEE Probe Anatomy — Why LA Is Always Best
The esophagus runs directly posterior to the LA, making the LA the nearest cardiac structure to the TEE transducer. This explains excellent views of: LAA (thrombus), mitral valve (en face from LA), all 4 pulmonary veins, posterior structures. The aortic root sits anterior — viewed "through" the LA in mid-esophageal windows. The TG position sees the LV from below via the stomach through the diaphragm.

Aortic Valve — Anatomy
  • Leaflets: Left coronary (LCC), Right coronary (RCC), Non-coronary (NCC)
  • Sinuses of Valsalva: Aortic root bulges behind each leaflet; LCA ostium from LCC sinus; RCA ostium from RCC sinus
  • Aortic annulus: Fibrous ring; surgical and TAVR implantation target
  • Bicuspid AV: 1–2% prevalence; LCC + RCC fusion most common; associated ascending aortopathy
  • TAVR sizing: CT-measured annular diameter; undersizing → paravalvular leak; oversizing → annular rupture
Aortic Stenosis — Anesthetic Approach
  • Severe AS: AVA <1.0 cm², Vmax >4 m/s, mean gradient >40 mmHg
  • Avoid tachycardia: Reduces diastolic filling time; increases O₂ demand in hypertrophied LV
  • Avoid sudden vasodilation: Critical afterload dependence; BP crash with spinal/epidural
  • Maintain SR: LV depends heavily on atrial kick (25–40% of filling in stiff LV)
  • TAVR: 15–30% new LBBB; 5–15% need permanent pacemaker (His bundle proximity)
Mitral Valve — Complex Anatomy
  • Anterior leaflet (A1–A3): "Aortic leaflet" — fibrous continuity with AV; larger but less annular attachment
  • Posterior leaflet (P1–P2–P3): Three scallops; P2 is most common site of MVP
  • Annulus: Saddle-shaped, non-planar — critical for accurate regurgitation grading by TEE
  • Anterolateral PM: Dual supply (LAD + LCx) — less ischemia susceptibility
  • Posteromedial PM: Single supply (RCA or LCx) — most prone to ischemic rupture
  • Chordae tendineae: Prevent leaflet prolapse into LA during systole; primary/secondary/tertiary
MR Jet Direction (TEE Diagnosis)
  • Posterior leaflet pathology (P2/P3 prolapse): Eccentric jet toward anterior wall / roof of LA
  • Anterior leaflet pathology (A1/A2 prolapse): Eccentric jet toward posterior wall of LA
  • Central jet: Functional MR, dilated cardiomyopathy, annular dilation
  • Flail leaflet: Loss of coaptation; leaflet tip visible in LA; severe MR
  • Acute MR (PM rupture): Hyperdynamic LV, small hyperdynamic LA, pulmonary edema pattern
⚠ Posteromedial Papillary Muscle Rupture — Surgical Emergency
Single-vessel blood supply → inferior MI (RCA territory) can cause acute posteromedial PM rupture → severe acute MR → cardiogenic shock. TEE: flail P3/A3 segment, severe eccentric MR jet toward the roof of the LA. This is a surgical emergency. Do NOT delay for medical optimization — proceed to emergent surgical repair or replacement.
Tricuspid Valve — Anatomy
  • Leaflets: Anterior (largest), Septal, Inferior (posterior)
  • Annulus: Borders the His bundle — surgical ring annuloplasty carries risk of complete heart block
  • Eustachian valve: Remnant fetal IVC valve in RA; may complicate TV procedures
  • Functional TR (most common): Not primary leaflet pathology — secondary to RV dilation → annular dilatation → leaflet tethering
  • Causes of TR: Pulmonary HTN, RV failure, IVDA endocarditis (veggie on TV), carcinoid, rheumatic
Managing TR in Anesthesia
  • Avoid increases in PVR: hypoxia, hypercarbia, acidosis, nitrous oxide
  • Maintain lower heart rates — TR worsens with tachycardia
  • Inhaled NO, prostaglandin E1, sildenafil: Reduce PVR in pulmonary HTN
  • Milrinone: Inodilator — increases RV inotropy + decreases PVR simultaneously
  • Vasopressin preferred over NE in combined pulmonary HTN + systemic hypotension (less PVR increase)
Pulmonic Valve — Anatomy
  • Leaflets: Anterior, Left, Right semilunar — three cusps
  • No subvalvular apparatus — purely pressure-driven open/close mechanism
  • Pulmonary HTN: PA dilation → pulmonic insufficiency → RV volume overload → RV dilation → functional TR cascade
  • Pulmonic stenosis: Congenital; systolic ejection murmur at left 2nd ICS; best viewed on TEE ME RV inflow-outflow view
  • Difficult to visualize on TEE — most anterior structure; highest failure rate for complete imaging
PVR — What Moves It
  • ↑ PVR (bad for RV): Hypoxia, hypercarbia, acidosis, N₂O, hypothermia, pain/light anesthesia, lung overdistension
  • ↓ PVR (helpful in PHT): Supplemental O₂, normocarbia, mild alkalosis, inhaled NO, prostaglandin E1 (PGE1), sildenafil (PDE5 inhibitor), milrinone
  • Optimize RV preload: Moderate volume loading; avoid both hypovolemia and overdistension (causes RV failure)

🔷 Intraoperative Ischemia — Triangulation Protocol
Correlate three data streams simultaneously:
ECG (lead configuration II/III/aVF vs. V4-V6 vs. anterior leads) + TEE (TG mid short-axis wall motion) + Hemodynamics (sudden BP drop, new dysrhythmia).

Example: New ST elevation II/III/aVF + new inferior hypokinesis on TG short-axis = RCA territory. This guides emergent hemodynamic management, vasopressor choice, and the decision for emergent CABG vs. continued surgery.

⚠ PA Catheter + Pre-existing LBBB = Complete Heart Block Risk
PA catheter passage through the RV can mechanically compress the right bundle branch causing transient RBBB. In patients with pre-existing LBBB, this creates iatrogenic complete heart block — potentially fatal.

Always: Have transcutaneous pacing pads applied and active before PA catheter insertion in LBBB patients. Consider placing a prophylactic transvenous pacing wire first in elective high-risk cases (severe LBBB + need for PA catheter).
🔷 TAVR Conduction Disturbances
TAVR carries a 15–30% risk of new LBBB and 5–15% permanent pacemaker requirement. The His bundle runs immediately adjacent to the aortic annulus at the NCC–RCC junction. External pacing must be immediately available throughout the procedure. Anticipate sudden complete heart block at balloon inflation or valve deployment — have a pacing-capable wire in the RV at all times during TAVR.
🔷 Moderator Band (Septomarginal Trabecula)
Carries the right bundle branch across the RV from the interventricular septum to the base of the anterior papillary muscle. This is why RBB injury from PA catheters occurs — the band lies directly in the path of the catheter traversing the RV. Visible on TEE in the RV: confirms correct orientation. Injury during right ventriculotomy or endomyocardial biopsy → RBBB.
High-Yield: SA node innate rate 60–100 → AV node 40–60 → His bundle/bundle branches 20–40 → Purkinje 20–40 bpm. Each successive pacemaker is progressively more escape and less reliable. Pacemaker hierarchy is the basis for understanding escape rhythms after cardiac arrest.
Cardiac Autonomic Innervation
Sympathetic and parasympathetic pathways — the pharmacologic foundation of perioperative cardiovascular management.
Sympathetic Innervation (T1–T4)
  • Preganglionic: T1–T4 spinal levels → sympathetic chain ganglia
  • Postganglionic fibers to: SA node, AV node, atria, ventricles, coronary arteries
  • SA node: ↑ HR (positive chronotropy)
  • AV node: ↑ conduction velocity (positive dromotropy)
  • Ventricles: ↑ contractility (positive inotropy)
  • Vessels: vasoconstriction (alpha-1 receptors)
  • Cervicothoracic (stellate) ganglion: Primary cardiac sympathetic hub; target for SGB in VT storm
Parasympathetic Innervation (Vagus CN X)
  • Preganglionic: Dorsal motor nucleus of vagus (medulla oblongata)
  • Cardiac branches: Superior + inferior cervical cardiac branches, thoracic cardiac branches
  • SA node: ↓ HR (negative chronotropy)
  • AV node: ↓ conduction velocity → AV block (negative dromotropy)
  • Ventricles: minimal direct inotropic effect
  • Neurotransmitter: Acetylcholine → M2 muscarinic receptor
⚠ High Neuraxial Block — Cardiac Sympathectomy
T1–T4 neuraxial block (high spinal or epidural) blocks the cardiac accelerator fibers → bradycardia + hypotension. Unmasks vagal tone → parasympathetic-dominant state. The higher the block, the more profound the chronotropic and inotropic depression. Asystole is possible.

Treatment: Epinephrine (not just atropine) for hemodynamic collapse. Atropine alone may be insufficient if sympathetic block is complete. Have vasopressors drawn up before induction of any neuraxial anesthetic.
🔷 Stellate Ganglion Block — Rescue Therapy for VT Storm
The cervicothoracic (stellate) ganglion block (SGB) has emerged as rescue therapy for refractory ventricular arrhythmia storm. By blocking left-sided cardiac sympathetic input, SGB reduces the triggered arrhythmia substrate.

Right SGB: Primarily affects SA node rate (reduces tachycardia)
Left SGB: Primarily affects ventricular arrhythmia threshold (reduces VT/VF burden)

Technique: Ultrasound-guided LA injection anterior to longus colli muscle at C6 (Chassaignac's tubercle). 10–20 mL local anesthetic. Confirm Horner's syndrome (ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis) = successful block.

⚠ Ductal-Dependent Congenital Heart Lesions
Hypoplastic left heart syndrome, critical aortic stenosis, interrupted aortic arch, pulmonary atresia — all require a patent ductus arteriosus for systemic or pulmonary blood flow to survive until surgical palliation.

Prostaglandin E1 (PGE1) infusion maintains ductal patency as a bridge. Critical side effect: APNEA (most dangerous — intubation equipment must be immediately available at all times). Also: fever, hypotension, flushing, seizures, cortical hyperostosis with long-term use.
🔷 PFO in Adult Anesthesia — Paradoxical Embolism
~25% of adults have a PFO that never anatomically sealed. Conditions that elevate RA pressure (high PEEP, PE, RV failure, Valsalva, prone positioning) can cause right-to-left shunting through the PFO → paradoxical embolism, refractory hypoxemia.

Beach-chair position with known PFO: risk of paradoxical air embolism (venous air → crosses PFO → systemic arterial circulation → stroke). Use precordial Doppler for VAE monitoring.
🔷 PDA Management in Premature Neonates
In premature neonates, the ductus arteriosus may fail to close → left-to-right shunt (systemic → pulmonary) → pulmonary overcirculation → congestive heart failure.

Medical: Indomethacin or ibuprofen (COX inhibitors → ↓ prostaglandins → DA constriction).
Surgical: PDA ligation if medical therapy fails — one of the highest-risk neonatal procedures. Anticipate: hemodynamic instability, temperature lability, metabolic acidosis, need for vasopressor support.
Head, Neck & Cerebral Vasculature
Carotid anatomy, Circle of Willis, jugular veins, and their relevance in neuroanesthesia, CEA, and regional techniques.
Common Carotid Bifurcation
  • Bifurcates at the upper border of the thyroid cartilage (C3–C4)
  • Carotid sinus: Dilation at bifurcation; baroreceptors (CN IX / Hering's nerve) → reflex bradycardia + hypotension when compressed or stimulated during CEA
  • Carotid body: Chemoreceptor adjacent to bifurcation → responds to hypoxia, hypercapnia, acidosis → ↑ respiratory drive
  • CEA: Surgeons inject the carotid sinus with local anesthetic to prevent hemodynamic reflexes during dissection and cross-clamp
Circle of Willis — Cerebral Collateral Anatomy
  • Complete circle in only 20–25% of patients — partial variants are common
  • Components: Bilateral A1 (ACA), Anterior communicating a., Bilateral posterior communicating a., Bilateral P1 (PCA), Basilar a. apex
  • Carotid cross-clamping at CEA → contralateral carotid supplies via anterior communicating artery
  • Posterior circulation contributes via posterior communicating arteries
  • Shunt need at CEA depends on Circle of Willis completeness
🔷 Carotid Endarterectomy (CEA) — Anesthesia Framework
Regional (cervical plexus block): Gold standard for neuromonitoring — real-time awake neurological assessment during test occlusion. Patient reports symptoms (weakness, speech change, confusion) within seconds of ischemia. Avoids GA-associated hemodynamic instability.

General anesthesia: NIRS (cerebral oximetry) or EEG for neuromonitoring. Maintain MAP 20% above baseline during cross-clamping to ensure collateral perfusion. Shunt if NIRS drops >20% or EEG shows ischemic changes. Use esmolol to control intraoperative hypertension.
